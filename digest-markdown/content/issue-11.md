**Issue #11 — May 29, 2026**

This issue is a love letter to restraint. The best classroom technology I encountered this month did one thing well and then disappeared — no dashboards demanding attention, no badges, no friction. The four pieces below all wrestle with the same question: how do we choose tools that serve the lesson instead of becoming the lesson? My summaries; the links carry the full case.

## #1 - The Tool That Disappears

> Image: a clean visual of a single uncluttered app screen with one button.

### The Big Idea
The most effective digital tools are the ones students stop noticing. The article frames "invisibility" as a design goal: the moment a tool calls attention to itself, it's competing with the learning instead of supporting it.

### Why It Matters
Cognitive load is finite. Every menu, popup, and setting a learner has to manage is attention borrowed from the actual task.

### Practical Takeaways
- Before adopting a tool, count the clicks to the core action — fewer is better.
- Prefer tools that work without an onboarding tour.
- Audit one app this month for features your class never uses.

**Source:** [Quiet Tools Review](https://www.example.org/tool-that-disappears)
**Author:** Dr. Iris Calderón

## #2 - Notifications Are a Curriculum

> Image: a clean visual of a phone face-down beside a stack of library books.

### The Big Idea
Every notification a platform sends teaches students what to value. The piece argues that default alerts quietly train attention toward whatever the vendor wants engaged with — not necessarily what we want learned.

### Why It Matters
Attention is the scarcest resource in any classroom. If a tool's notifications fragment it, the tool is working against you no matter how good its features are.

### Practical Takeaways
- Turn off non-essential notifications as part of setup, not as an afterthought.
- Discuss alert design with students as a media-literacy lesson.
- Choose tools that let *you* control the cadence of reminders.

**Source:** [Attention Ledger](https://www.example.org/notifications-curriculum)
**Author:** Bram Henley

## #3 - One Login Is a Feature

> Image: a clean visual of a single key on a plain keyring.

### The Big Idea
Tool sprawl has a hidden cost: every separate account is a small barrier that compounds across a day. The article makes the unglamorous case that consolidation and single sign-on are pedagogical wins, not just IT conveniences.

### Why It Matters
Minutes lost to password resets and account-juggling are minutes not spent learning — and the friction falls hardest on the students with the least support at home.

### Practical Takeaways
- Favor tools that integrate with your existing sign-on.
- Retire one redundant app each term to fight sprawl.
- Track how many logins a typical lesson actually requires.

**Source:** [Frictionless Schools](https://www.example.org/one-login-feature)
**Author:** Nadia Voss

## #4 - When "No Tech" Is the Right Tech

> Image: a clean visual of an index card and a pencil on a wooden desk.

### The Big Idea
The most countercultural move in this issue: sometimes the best tool is paper. The article isn't anti-technology; it's pro-fit, arguing we should reach for the lightest tool that accomplishes the goal.

### Why It Matters
Defaulting to the screen can crowd out exactly the slow, tactile thinking some lessons need. Choosing *not* to digitize is itself a design decision worth making on purpose.

### Practical Takeaways
- Ask "what would this look like with no device?" before adding one.
- Reserve screens for tasks they uniquely enable.
- Mix analog and digital deliberately rather than by habit.

**Source:** [Right-Sized Learning](https://www.example.org/no-tech-right-tech)
**Author:** Caleb Ofori

## Closing Reflection

If there's a takeaway across these four, it's that good tool choices are mostly subtraction. Fewer clicks, fewer alerts, fewer logins, fewer screens-by-default. The work is in saying no often enough that the few tools we keep can actually disappear into the learning. This week I'm cutting one app. It won't be missed.

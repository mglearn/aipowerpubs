## #1 - Attention Is Built, Not Grabbed

> Image: a clean visual of a sprout breaking through soil in morning light.

### The Big Idea
We talk about "grabbing" attention as if it were a resource to seize. The article reframes it as a *capacity* learners develop over time — more like a muscle than a switch.

### Why It Matters
If attention is built, then constant novelty and stimulation may be training exactly the wrong thing: the expectation that focus should be effortless and externally supplied.

### Practical Takeaways
- Protect short stretches of single-task work and grow them gradually.
- Name attention as a skill students can practice, not a trait they have or lack.
- Resist the urge to add stimulation when engagement dips — try subtracting instead.

**Source:** [Focus & Learning Review](https://www.example.org/attention-is-built)
**Author:** Dr. Lena Marquez

## #2 - The Myth of Multitasking Students

> Image: a clean visual of two overlapping clocks slightly out of sync.

### The Big Idea
The piece dismantles the comforting belief that today's learners are uniquely wired to multitask. What looks like parallel work is rapid, costly switching — and the cost is paid in comprehension.

### Why It Matters
Designing lessons around the assumption of seamless multitasking quietly lowers the depth of thinking we ask for. Naming the myth lets us design for focus honestly.

### Practical Takeaways
- Build "one tab" norms for tasks that need depth.
- Show students the switching cost with a quick in-class demo.
- Sequence tasks instead of stacking them.

**Source:** [Cognition Notes](https://www.example.org/multitasking-myth)
**Author:** Devon Achterberg

## #3 - Designing for the First Five Minutes

> Image: a clean visual of an open door with light spilling into a quiet room.

### The Big Idea
The opening of a lesson sets the attentional tone for everything after it. The article argues for treating the first five minutes as a deliberate on-ramp rather than logistics-and-housekeeping time.

### Why It Matters
Attention has inertia. A scattered start is hard to recover from; a focused one carries learners through the harder middle.

### Practical Takeaways
- Open with one concrete question instead of announcements.
- Move housekeeping to the end where it costs less focus.
- Use a consistent ritual so the brain knows "we're starting now."

**Source:** [Lesson Design Quarterly](https://www.example.org/first-five-minutes)
**Author:** Priya Sundaram

## #4 - Boredom Has a Job to Do

> Image: a clean visual of a person gazing out a rain-streaked window.

### The Big Idea
The most provocative read of the issue: boredom isn't a failure of engagement but a precursor to it. The article suggests that always-on stimulation deprives learners of the restless space where curiosity and original thinking start.

### Why It Matters
If we engineer away every dull moment, we may also engineer away the daydreaming that lets ideas connect. A little boredom is generative.

### Practical Takeaways
- Leave unstructured thinking time without rushing to fill it.
- Reframe "I'm bored" as an invitation rather than an emergency.
- Resist the impulse to gamify every quiet stretch.

**Source:** [The Slow Classroom](https://www.example.org/boredom-has-a-job)
**Author:** Yusuf El-Amin

## Closing Reflection

Ten issues in, this is the one I'll keep coming back to. Attention isn't a prize we win from learners with brighter, faster, louder lessons — it's a capacity we either cultivate or erode with every design choice. This week I'm guarding the first five minutes and leaving a little room for boredom. Counterintuitive, maybe. Worth trying.

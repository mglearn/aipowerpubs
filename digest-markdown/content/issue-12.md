## #1 - Feedback That Arrives in Time

> Image: a clean visual of a stopwatch resting on an open notebook, soft daylight.

### The Big Idea
Feedback loses most of its value the moment it goes cold. The piece argues that *timing* outranks *thoroughness*: a short note delivered while the work is still warm beats an essay of corrections returned two weeks later.

### Why It Matters
When response is delayed, students have already moved on — and so have their misconceptions, now quietly reinforced. Fast, lightweight feedback keeps the learner in a position to actually act on it.

### Practical Takeaways
- Aim for a 48-hour response window on formative work, even if the note is brief.
- Replace one long end-of-unit comment with three short mid-unit ones.
- Use a quick voice memo or margin note instead of a polished paragraph.

**Source:** [Timely Practice Review](https://www.example.org/feedback-in-time)
**Author:** Dr. Renata Foss

## #2 - The Comment Nobody Reads

> Image: a clean visual of a returned paper with untouched margin notes.

### The Big Idea
Most written feedback is never used, not because students don't care, but because it's framed as a verdict rather than a next step. Reframing comments as a single actionable question changes whether they get read at all.

### Why It Matters
If feedback isn't acted on, it isn't feedback — it's just commentary. Shifting from judgment to invitation turns a closed loop into an open one.

### Practical Takeaways
- End each comment with one question the student can answer in a revision.
- Limit yourself to the two highest-leverage notes per piece.
- Ask students to reply to your comment before they revise.

**Source:** [The Margins Journal](https://www.example.org/comment-nobody-reads)
**Author:** Theo Marwick

## #3 - Peer Review Without the Pile-Up

> Image: a clean visual of two students trading drafts across a small table.

### The Big Idea
Peer review fails when it's vague ("looks good!") or crushing ("rewrite everything"). Structured prompts that ask reviewers to *notice* before they *judge* produce feedback both sides can use.

### Why It Matters
Done well, peer review multiplies the amount of timely response a learner gets while teaching the reviewer to read critically — a two-for-one that no single teacher can scale alone.

### Practical Takeaways
- Give reviewers a sentence stem: "One thing that worked for me was…"
- Cap each review at three observations to prevent overwhelm.
- Have authors mark *what kind* of feedback they want before sharing.

**Source:** [Collaborative Classroom Notes](https://www.example.org/peer-review-pileup)
**Author:** Mireille Adebayo

## #4 - Rubrics as Conversation Starters

> Image: a clean visual of a simple rubric pinned beside a whiteboard.

### The Big Idea
A rubric isn't a scoring machine; it's a shared vocabulary. The article makes the case for co-building rubrics with students so the criteria become something they can talk about, not just be measured against.

### Why It Matters
When learners help name what quality looks like, they can self-assess long before the work reaches you — closing the feedback loop from the inside.

### Practical Takeaways
- Draft one rubric row together as a class before an assignment.
- Translate jargon into student language ("clear" beats "demonstrates clarity").
- Let students annotate their own work against the rubric before submitting.

**Source:** [Assessment Dialogues](https://www.example.org/rubrics-conversation)
**Author:** Sam Okonkwo

## Closing Reflection

The thread running through all four reads: feedback is a relationship, not a transaction. Speed, specificity, and shared language each turn a one-way verdict into a back-and-forth a learner can grow inside. This week I'm picking one class and trying the 48-hour rule. We'll see what loosens.
